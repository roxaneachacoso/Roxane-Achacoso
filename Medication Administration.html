The 10 Rights of Medication Administration

A Comprehensive Guide to Promoting Safe and Effective Drug Delivery in Clinical Practice

Medication errors remain a critical issue in healthcare, with administration errors accounting for up to 60% of all drug-related mistakes (Institute of Medicine, 2006). These errors can cause serious consequences including harm, increased costs, and even death.

Traditionally guided by the “Five Rights,” today’s best practices embrace an expanded and more holistic framework: the “Ten Rights” of medication administration. This approach prioritizes patient safety, critical thinking, and professional accountability.

1. Right Patient

Always verify the identity of the correct patient before administering any medication.

  • Match name and hospital ID with the medication order
  • Use two or more identifiers (e.g., full name and DOB)
  • Use barcode scanning if available
Why it matters: Misidentification can result in catastrophic consequences, especially in high-risk settings.

2. Right Medication

Ensure the correct drug is administered and avoid look-alike, sound-alike medications.

  • Compare medication label with MAR three times
  • Check generic names and expiration dates
  • Be alert to packaging or color changes
WHO considers look-alike/sound-alike medication errors a major global concern.

3. Right Dose

Confirm that the correct amount is being administered based on guidelines and patient needs.

  • Double-check weight-based or high-risk calculations
  • Validate unusual doses with a second nurse
Dosing errors can lead to toxicity, inefficacy, or long-term harm.

4. Right Route

Verify the route (oral, IV, inhaled, etc.) and ensure the patient can tolerate it safely.

  • Never switch routes without an order
  • Understand route-specific effects and risks
Different routes result in different absorption and bioavailability.

5. Right Time

Administer medications at the correct time intervals as prescribed.

  • Be mindful of food interactions and time-sensitive meds
  • Adjust timing based on previous dose and therapeutic range
Timing errors are among the most common in long-term care facilities.

6. Right Patient Education

Inform patients about their medications, uses, and side effects.

  • Provide both verbal and written information
  • Encourage questions and feedback
Educated patients are more compliant and safety-aware.

7. Right Documentation

Ensure timely and accurate documentation of drug administration.

  • Never document before giving medication
  • Include dose, time, site, response, or refusal
Inaccurate documentation can cause harmful duplications or omissions.

8. Right to Refuse

Patients have the legal right to refuse medications.

  • Respect patient autonomy while providing full information
  • Document refusal and notify the physician
Supporting patient choice is an ethical and professional obligation.

9. Right Assessment

Evaluate the patient’s condition and appropriateness of the drug.

  • Check vitals or labs before giving medications
  • Know when PRN meds are necessary
Proper assessment prevents adverse drug reactions and complications.

10. Right Evaluation

After administration, assess therapeutic effect and side effects.

  • Continue monitoring, especially with critical meds
  • Communicate any issues promptly
Ongoing evaluation is essential for medication safety and patient outcomes.

Summary: Why the Ten Rights Matter

The Ten Rights of Medication Administration create a safety net for both patients and nurses. They reduce medication errors, support patient autonomy, and promote better outcomes through informed, evidence-based, and ethical practice.
