Basic Nursing Knowledge

The Nursing Process (ADPIE)

The Nursing Process is a systematic, patient-centered, evidence-based method used by nurses to ensure quality and individualized care. It serves as the core framework for clinical decision-making and critical thinking in nursing practice. The acronym ADPIE outlines the five essential steps: Assessment, Diagnosis, Planning, Implementation, and Evaluation. This cyclical process allows for continuous improvement in patient care and adaptation to changing patient needs.

1. Assessment

Definition: Assessment is the first and foundational step of the nursing process. It involves the systematic collection, verification, and organization of data about the patient’s health status.

Key Elements:

  • Subjective Data: Information shared by the patient such as symptoms, feelings, and perceptions (e.g., pain level, fatigue, emotional distress).
  • Objective Data: Observable and measurable facts, including vital signs, lab results, and findings from physical assessments.
  • Primary Sources: Data obtained directly from the patient.
  • Secondary Sources: Information from family members, caregivers, medical records, or other healthcare providers.

Purpose: To build a holistic understanding of the patient’s physiological, psychological, sociocultural, developmental, and spiritual health.

Tools and Techniques:

  • Head-to-toe physical examinations
  • Health history interviews
  • Diagnostic tests and screenings
  • Standardized assessment tools (e.g., Braden Scale, Glasgow Coma Scale)

2. Diagnosis

Definition: The diagnosis step involves analyzing and interpreting assessment data to determine the patient’s problems, which may be actual or at risk of developing.

Types of Nursing Diagnoses:

  • Actual Diagnosis: A current health issue confirmed by signs and symptoms.
  • Risk Diagnosis: A potential problem that may develop based on risk factors.
  • Health Promotion Diagnosis: Focused on improving wellness in individuals who are already healthy or are at a high level of functioning.

Process:

  • Use of clinical judgment to identify priorities.
  • Application of NANDA-I taxonomy to name and classify diagnoses.
  • Development of a problem statement, etiology, and defining characteristics.

Example:

  • Actual: Impaired Gas Exchange related to alveolar-capillary membrane changes as evidenced by oxygen saturation of 86% on room air.
  • Risk: Risk for Falls related to unsteady gait and history of falls.

3. Planning

Definition: Planning involves setting measurable and achievable short- and long-term goals based on the patient’s nursing diagnoses. It includes the selection of evidence-based interventions that aim to improve health outcomes.

Components:

  • Prioritization of problems (using Maslow’s Hierarchy of Needs or ABCs – Airway, Breathing, Circulation).
  • SMART Goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-bound.
  • Care Planning: Development of a written care plan that guides the nursing team and ensures continuity of care.

Example Goal: Patient will maintain oxygen saturation above 92% on room air within 48 hours.

4. Implementation

Definition: This step involves executing the care plan through specific nursing actions and interventions, either independent, dependent, or collaborative.

Types of Interventions:

  • Independent: Actions the nurse can perform without a provider’s order (e.g., repositioning the patient, providing education).
  • Dependent: Actions that require a physician’s order (e.g., administering medications).
  • Collaborative: Interventions carried out in conjunction with other healthcare professionals (e.g., physical therapy referrals, discharge planning).

Documentation: All interventions must be accurately recorded to ensure legal accountability and continuity of care.

Examples:

  • Administering prescribed oxygen therapy.
  • Educating the patient on deep breathing exercises.
  • Monitoring respiratory rate and oxygen saturation every 2 hours.

5. Evaluation

Definition: Evaluation determines whether the goals set in the planning phase were achieved. It involves reassessing the patient’s status and the effectiveness of interventions.

Process:

  • Comparing actual outcomes with expected outcomes.
  • Determining the success or failure of the nursing care plan.
  • Modifying or revising the care plan based on the patient’s current status and response to interventions.

Possible Outcomes:

  • Goal Met: No further action needed or continue current plan.
  • Goal Partially Met: Modify existing interventions or goals.
  • Goal Not Met: Reassess and develop a new plan of care.

Importance of the Nursing Process

  • Ensures individualized and holistic care.
  • Promotes evidence-based practice.
  • Supports patient safety and continuity of care.
  • Fosters professional accountability and critical thinking.
  • Aligns with legal and ethical standards of nursing practice.
