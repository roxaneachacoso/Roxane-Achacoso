The 10 Rights of Medication Administration

A Comprehensive Guide to Promoting Safe and Effective Drug Delivery in Clinical Practice

Medication errors remain a critical issue in healthcare, with administration errors accounting for up to 60% of all drug-related mistakes (Institute of Medicine, 2006). These errors can cause serious consequences including harm, increased costs, and even death.

Traditionally guided by the “Five Rights,” today’s best practices embrace an expanded and more holistic framework: the “Ten Rights” of medication administration. This approach prioritizes patient safety, critical thinking, and professional accountability.

1. Right Patient

Always verify the identity of the correct patient before administering any medication.

  • Match name and hospital ID with the medication order
  • Use two or more identifiers (e.g., full name and DOB)
  • Use barcode scanning if available
Why it matters: Misidentification can result in catastrophic consequences, especially in high-risk settings.

2. Right Medication

Ensure the correct drug is administered and avoid look-alike, sound-alike medications.

  • Compare medication label with MAR three times
  • Check generic names and expiration dates
  • Be alert to packaging or color changes
WHO considers look-alike/sound-alike medication errors a major global concern.

3. Right Dose

Confirm that the correct amount is being administered based on guidelines and patient needs.

  • Double-check weight-based or high-risk calculations
  • Validate unusual doses with a second nurse
Dosing errors can lead to toxicity, inefficacy, or long-term harm.

4. Right Route

Verify the route (oral, IV, inhaled, etc.) and ensure the patient can tolerate it safely.

  • Never switch routes without an order
  • Understand route-specific effects and risks
Different routes result in different absorption and bioavailability.

5. Right Time

Administer medications at the correct time intervals as prescribed.

  • Be mindful of food interactions and time-sensitive meds
  • Adjust timing based on previous dose and therapeutic range
Timing errors are among the most common in long-term care facilities.

6. Right Patient Education

Inform patients about their medications, uses, and side effects.

  • Provide both verbal and written information
  • Encourage questions and feedback
Educated patients are more compliant and safety-aware.

7. Right Documentation

Ensure timely and accurate documentation of drug administration.

  • Never document before giving medication
  • Include dose, time, site, response, or refusal
Inaccurate documentation can cause harmful duplications or omissions.

8. Right to Refuse

Patients have the legal right to refuse medications.

  • Respect patient autonomy while providing full information
  • Document refusal and notify the physician
Supporting patient choice is an ethical and professional obligation.

9. Right Assessment

Evaluate the patient’s condition and appropriateness of the drug.

  • Check vitals or labs before giving medications
  • Know when PRN meds are necessary
Proper assessment prevents adverse drug reactions and complications.

10. Right Evaluation

After administration, assess therapeutic effect and side effects.

  • Continue monitoring, especially with critical meds
  • Communicate any issues promptly
Ongoing evaluation is essential for medication safety and patient outcomes.

Summary: Why the Ten Rights Matter

The Ten Rights of Medication Administration create a safety net for both patients and nurses. They reduce medication errors, support patient autonomy, and promote better outcomes through informed, evidence-based, and ethical practice.

7 Ethical Principles in Nursing

All nurses, regardless of practice area, make decisions in the course of their daily duties that require ethical consideration. But as any nurse will tell you, these decisions can weigh heavily on a person’s shoulders. A core set of ethical principles in nursing offers a solid foundation that nurses can rely on to help them make sound decisions. Such ethical principles likely contribute to the fact that, historically, Americans trust nurses more than any other profession, according to Gallup’s annual Honesty and Ethics poll.

Principles of Ethics in Nursing: Background

Ethics refers to how people think and conduct themselves in terms of right and wrong. The concept of nursing ethics began to develop in the late 19th century. At the time, ethics in nursing referred to certain character traits like obedience, physician loyalty, and high moral character.

As the world and the healthcare industry evolves, so too must nursing ethics themselves. Nurses face increasingly complex human rights and ethical issues. In response, the American Nurses Association developed its Code of Ethics for Nurses, which serves as the standard by which all nurses should practice. It acts as a social contract between nurses and the general public, helping people understand the commitment all nurses make to providing safe, evidence-based, high-quality care.

Each member of the profession adheres to a set of core ethical principles in nursing. The nursing code of ethics helps nurses make good judgment calls, provide high-quality care, and support patients and their loved ones emotionally, physically, and mentally. It also helps keep a nurse’s license in good standing.

What Are the 7 Ethical Principles in Nursing?

  • Accountability
  • Justice
  • Nonmaleficence
  • Autonomy
  • Beneficence
  • Fidelity
  • Veracity

1. Accountability

Each nurse must be responsible for his or her own choices and actions in the course of patient care. Nurses who hold themselves accountable often provide higher-quality patient care and build stronger relationships with patients.

2. Justice

Nurses must make care decisions based only on the facts—not on factors like age, ethnicity, religion, socioeconomic status, or sexual orientation. Acting as fair and impartial caregivers ensures that each patient is treated with dignity and equity.

3. Nonmaleficence

Nurses must take action to prevent harm. This principle—“do no harm”—is fundamental to safe, effective care and a nurse’s responsibility.

4. Autonomy

Patients have the right to make their own decisions. Nurses must support this right, even when a patient's choice differs from medical advice, as long as informed consent is given.

5. Beneficence

This principle means acting in the best interest of the patient. Nurses must practice kindness and charity, always considering the unique needs and situations of each person they care for.

6. Fidelity

Fidelity means keeping promises. Trust and loyalty between a nurse and patient help build strong, therapeutic relationships and ensure effective care.

7. Veracity

Even when the truth is difficult to share, nurses must be honest. This builds trust, empowers patients, and ensures informed decisions.

Nursing Ethical Principles in Practice

Most nursing programs include ethics training. These principles are often applied naturally in real-life situations. Here are some practical examples:

  • Accountability: Discarding incorrect medication instead of giving it to the patient.
  • Justice: Advocating for a patient in need of financial or disability-related support.
  • Nonmaleficence: Always double-checking medication, even for familiar patients.
  • Autonomy: Respecting a patient’s care decision, even when you disagree clinically.
  • Beneficence: Helping a patient weigh the pros and cons of treatment options.
  • Fidelity: Keeping a promise of confidentiality to your patient.
  • Veracity: Telling a patient the full truth about their condition with empathy.

SBAR: Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation

SBAR is a structured communication framework widely used in healthcare settings to facilitate concise, focused, and effective conversations among team members. It stands for Situation, Background, Assessment, and Recommendation (or Request).

  • Promotes clarity during critical conversations.
  • Allows all team members, including nurses, physicians, and specialists, to speak up with confidence.
  • Essential in emergencies, deteriorating patient conditions, or discussions with external consultants.

Steps of SBAR

1. Situation

Clearly describe what is currently happening. Include your name, role, the patient’s name, age, gender, and the immediate issue.

2. Background

Share relevant clinical background: medical history, presenting symptoms, past treatments, recent lab or diagnostic results.

3. Assessment

Explain your clinical judgment. What do you think is going on? Provide a working diagnosis if possible or describe signs and symptoms and their implications.

4. Recommendation / Request

Clearly state what you need, when you need it, and confirm understanding by repeating the response you receive.

Tips for Using SBAR Effectively

  • Always introduce yourself.
  • Use actual SBAR phrases like: "The situation is...", "The background is..." to stay on track.
  • Adapt the structure based on the urgency and context.
  • SBAR can be taught to patients and families to help them communicate with the care team.
SBAR: Sample Phrases and Applications
Step Purpose Starter Phrase
Situation Confirm understanding of the patient’s current symptoms. "I want to confirm my understanding of your symptoms… [list symptoms]. Is there something I missed?"
Background Acknowledge symptom impact on the patient’s life. "Your symptoms are impacting you by… [explain]. Is there anything else I should know?"
Assessment Share working diagnosis and invite concerns. "My initial thinking is that your symptoms are consistent with… [diagnosis]. What is most concerning for you?"
Recommendation / Request Propose the next steps clearly and confirm patient understanding. "I would like you to have [test/treatment]. You should complete it within [timeframe] and follow up after."
